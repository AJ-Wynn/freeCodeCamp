Dr. Maurice Hilleman

The man whose work is estimated to save around eight million lives every year

Maurice Hilleman, PhD’44, and his flu research team at the Walter Reed Army Institute of Research in 1957.

Early Life

  • 1919 - born on a farm near Miles City, Montana. His twin sister died during the birth, and his mother died a few days later.
  • 1927 - eight year-old Maurice nearly died from diphtheria, which would later be virtually eradicated in the US through immunization.

Education

  • 1941 - graduated from Montana State University, first in his class and with a degree in chemistry and microbiology. Won a full scholarship to begin graduate studies in microbiology at the University of Chicago.
  • 1944 - received his doctoral degree in microbiology. Hilleman established that Chlamydia is an unusually shaped bacterium that grows only inside of cells, not a virus as had been thought.

Career

  • 1944 - while working at E. R. Squibb and Sons, helped develop a Japanese encephalitis vaccine to protect American troops in the World War II Pacific theater of operations.
  • 1948 - appointed as chief of the Department of Respiratory Diseases at the Army Medical Center (renamed the Walter Reed Army Institute of Research in 1953).
  • 1957 - becomes director of Virus and Cell Biology Research for the Merck Institute for Therapeutic Research.
  • 1962 - Maurice Hilleman and Eugene Buynak isolate the Benoit strain of rubella virus.
  • 1963 - isolates the mumps virus from his daughter, Jeryl Lynn, during her illness.
  • 1968 - manufactured a vaccine to combat a new influenza A virus, Type A2, Hong Kong strain, that had caused widespread illness in Hong Kong.
  • 1971 - U.S. government licenses Merck’s combined trivalent measles, mumps, and rubella vaccine (MMR).
  • 1981 - Merck attenuates the Oka strain of chickenpox virus (varicella-zoster virus) that would later be used in the licensed chickenpox vaccine.

Legacy

  • 1984 - officially retires from Merck but maintains regular office hours there for many years and served as an advisor to the World Health Organization.
  • 1988 - President Ronald Regan awards him the National Medal of Science, the highest science award given in the U.S.
  • 2005 - dies at the age of 85.
